XY Planar Slice

These toolpaths generate planar passes by slicing the model with the XY plane of the defined Tool Plane. These planar passes run at a constant Z value as they follow the model profile. These toolpaths typically perform well on steep areas of models as the distance between planar passes can vary when run along shallow features. However, advanced options are supported to improve results on shallow areas.

XY Planar Slice Toolpaths consist of the following toolpaths:

  • Surface Finish Contour

  • Surface Rough Contour

  • 3D HST Waterline

Toolpath Guidelines

Before you create an XY Planar toolpath, consider the following:

  • 3D High Speed operations allow you to select machining geometry and avoidance geometry. Each pass is machined in the opposite direction to the previous pass.

  • Surface operations allow you to select drive, check and containment geometry.

Types of XY Planar Slice operations

There are three toolpaths in the XY Planar Slice toolpath group.. There are two toolpaths for finishing and one for roughing. All toolpaths run at a constant Z value. All toolpaths slice with the XY model down the defined Tool Plane, but the defined Tool Plane depends on your chosen toolpath.

Waterline

The 3D High Speed Waterline toolpaths are created from a set of profile curves along the cut surfaces. The profiles are separated by a constant Z amount. They are similar to finish contour toolpaths, but use Mastercam's high speed toolpath techniques for smoother, more efficient tool motion. They are typically used in finishing and semi-finishing operations.

Waterline toolpaths are best suited for surfaces whose angles are between 30 and 90 degrees. This is because the distance between passes is measured along the tool axis. Where the surfaces are shallower, material typically won't be removed as efficiently. However, you can configure the toolpath to generate extra cuts in shallow or flat areas.

Contour Toolpath Surface

Surface Rough/Surface Finish Contour toolpaths work well for parts with steep walls. The rough and finish contour toolpaths allow the tool to stepdown gradually in the Z axis instead of stepping over in the X and Y axes.

What are the differences between the toolpaths?

Group

3D high speed Waterline belongs to the 3D high speed toolpaths and Surface Finish Contour and Surface Rough Contour belong to the surface finish/rough toolpaths. The toolpath group affects parameters, geometry selection, and cutting speed.

Location

Access 3D HST Waterline, by clicking the Milling tab and selecting the operation from the 3D gallery.

To access Surface Finish/Rough Contour, right click in the Toolpaths Manager and select the toolpath from the Mill toolpaths, Surface Finish or Surface Rough menu

.

Geometry Selection

Geometry selection for 3D high speed Waterline is similar to other 3D HST operations. After selecting the toolpath from the ribbon, the toolpath parameters dialog box opens. Use Model Geometry and Toolpath Control pages in the toolpath dialog box to set machining regions and containment boundaries.

For the surface finish/rough operations, Mastercam prompts you to select drive geometry, check geometry, and a tool containment boundary. You can also select an approximate starting point. This process is similar to the selection process for all surface finish/rough operations.

Parameters

  • 3D high speed Waterline includes a Cut Order option on the Cut Parameters page. This allows you to order cuts by depth or to optimize them.

  • Surface Finish contour, Surface Rough contour and 3D HST Waterline operations include Cut tolerance, the Line/arc tolerance, and the Smoothing tolerance on the Total tolerance tab. Use this setting to set parameters for filtering and smoothing toolpath motion within the specified total toolpath tolerance.

Similar to how XY Planar Slice operations share gemoetry selection process of their toolpath group, XY Planar slice operations also share parameters with other toolpaths in their group.

  • The 3D high speed toolpaths have more options for Cutting method than the Surface Finish and Rough operations. Other 3D high speed toolpaths share these advanced options.

  • 3D HST Waterline operations have advanced linking parameter options, to give you more control over leads, retracts, and transitions. You can access these options on the Linking Parameters page.

  • The surface finish and rough contour operations include a Gap Settings dialog box that allows you to control what happens when a break in tool motion occurs.

  • To control the minimum and maximum machining depth for the toolpath, most Surface Finish operations use the Depth Limits dialog box. Similarly, most 3D HST toolpaths allow you to enter a maximum and minimum Z depth value on the Steep/Shallow page to establish the heights of the highest and lowest cutting passes. Learn more in Using Z Limits to Create a Machining Zone.

What are the similarities between the toolpaths?

In addition to all being pattern-projection toolpaths, the following parameters are shared between the toolpath types:

  • Surface Rough contour, Finish contour and 3D HST Waterline include Direction of closed contour on the toolpath parameter tab. This option orients the cutting direction of closed contours. Closed contours contain continuous motion without retractions, reversals, or direction.

  • These toolpaths also contain Direction of open contours. This orients the cutting direction of the open contours by selecting one of two options, One Way or Zigzag.

How do I decide between using a 3D high speed toolpath or a Surface toolpath?

When deciding between using a high speed toolpath or a Surface Rough or Finish toolpath, consider the following:

  • Toolpaths may have options and parameters that are unique to their group. For example, Surface toolpaths have more options for entering/exiting the part, and 3D high speed toolpaths have more options for linking parameters.

  • 3D HST toolpaths support holder collision checking when needed.

  • 3D HST toolpaths better support the use of stock models when needed.
