Pencil Toolpaths

Pencil toolpaths are useful for cleaning out the remaining material in the corners of a part after a machining operation. The tool follows a contour that is defined by the intersection of two or more surfaces. Bi-tangency contact occurs between the tool’s flutes and the corner, meaning the tool contacts the part in two locations as it runs along the contour.

Pencil operations place the tool against either:

  • Normal corners

  • Corners that contain a fillet. For fillets with a radius greater than the defined tool radius, use the Overthickness parameter to make the tool seem larger than it really is.

In the following image, a 3D HST Pencil toolpath clears out the remaining material in tight corners of the part.

Toolpath Guidelines

Before you create a Pencil toolpath, consider the following:

  • Pencil operations are finishing toolpaths. In most cases, it is best practice to use them after roughing operations are complete.

  • 3D High Speed Pencil operations allow you to select machining geometry and avoidance geometry. Surface Pencil operations allow you to select drive, check, and containment geometry.

Types of Pencil Operations

Mastercam offers two types of Pencil toolpaths:

Surface Finish Pencil 3D High Speed Pencil

3D High Speed Pencil and Surface Pencil both travel along the edges between intersecting surfaces to remove material from the stock. Depending on your machine, purpose, and the features on your part, one operation type may be more suited to your needs. For more information, see What are the differences between the toolpaths?

What are the differences between the toolpaths?

High Speed Pencil toolpaths are similar to Mastercam's standard Pencil toolpaths, but are enhanced to produce the smoother, free-flowing tool motion and transitions necessary for high speed machining.

Group

3D HST Pencil belongs with the 3D high speed toolpaths and Surface Finish Pencil belongs with the surface finish/rough toolpaths. The toolpath group affects the parameters, geometry selection, and cutting speed.

Location

Access 3D HST Pencil by clicking the Milling tab and selecting the operation from the 3D gallery. To access Surface Finish Pencil, right-click in the Toolpaths Manager and select Mill toolpaths, Surface finish, Pencil.

Geometry Selection

Geometry selection for 3D HST Pencil is similar to the other 3D HST operations. After selecting the toolpath from the ribbon, the toolpath parameters dialog box opens. Use the Model Geometry and Toolpath Control pages in the toolpath dialog box to set machining regions and containment boundaries.

For Surface Finish Pencil operations, Mastercam prompts you to select drive geometry, check geometry, and a tool containment boundary. You can also select an approximate starting point. This process is similar to the geometry selection process for all Surface Finish/Rough operations.

Parameters

The following parameters are unique to each type of Pencil operation:

  • Surface Finish Pencil operations include the Parallel passes option on the Finish pencil parameters page. Use this setting to create multiple passes that are offset by a stepover distance.

  • 3D HST Pencil operations include the Reference tool diameter on the Cut parameters page. This value represents the size of the theoretical roughing tool that would have machined the cutting zone defined for the toolpath. Use it as a guide to make sure that you are in fact machining all of the areas that were missed by your roughing tool. Reference tool diameter is only available when you use multiple offsets. See Reference Tool Diameter for Pencil Toolpaths to learn more.

Similar to how Pencil operations share the geometry selection process of their toolpath group, Pencil operations also share parameters with other toolpaths in their group.

  • 3D HST Pencil operations have more options for Cutting method than Surface Finish Pencil operations. Other 3D high speed toolpaths share these advanced options.

  • 3D HST Pencil operations have advanced linking parameter options, to give you more control over leads, retracts, and transitions. You can access these options on the Linking Parameters page.

  • Surface Finish Pencil operations have Gap Settings dialog box that allow you to control what happens when a break in tool motion occurs in a Surface toolpath.

  • To control the minimum and maximum machining depth for the toolpath, most Surface Finish operations use Depth Limits dialog box. Similarly, most 3D HST toolpaths allow you to enter a maximum and minimum Z depth value on the Steep / Shallow page to establish the heights of the highest and lowest cutting passes. Learn more in Using Z Limits to Create a Machining Zone.

What are the similarities between the toolpaths?

Both types of Pencil operations can have single or multiple passes. For each pass, Mastercam drives the cutting tool tangent to two surfaces at a time, cleaning out the remaining material between the surfaces.

The following parameters are only used in the two types of Pencil operations:

  • Overthickness is a value that Mastercam adds to the diameter of the tool radius during calculations for the purpose of identifying desired corner features that might be slightly larger the actual tool radius.

  • Bitangency angle specifies how shallow the corner or angle between two surfaces needs to be in order for Mastercam to create a cutting pass.

How do I decide between using a 3D high speed toolpath or a Surface toolpath?

When deciding between using a high speed toolpath or a Surface Rough or Finish toolpath, consider the following:

  • Toolpaths may have options and parameters that are unique to their group. For example, Surface toolpaths have more options for entering/exiting the part, and 3D high speed toolpaths have more options for linking parameters.

  • 3D HST toolpaths support holder collision checking when needed.

  • 3D HST toolpaths better support the use of stock models when needed.
