Blend Toolpaths

Mastercam's 2D Blend Mill, Surface Finish Blend, and 3D high speed Blend toolpaths use the input chains and optional model data to create a smooth machining pattern. When creating a Blend toolpath, you choose between 2D or 3D projection mode.

2D mode is a Z-based projection. Mastercam generates a flat pattern by blending the shape of the first chain to the shape of the second chain at a defined Stepover and projects the pattern down the Z axis of the tool plane onto the model.

3D mode is an on-surface 3D offset calculation. Mastercam first projects the selected chains down the Z axis of the tool plane onto the model to establish the chain location. Then, Mastercam calculates the offset passes from the first projected chain to the second projected chain, measuring the distance between the offset passes along the topology of the model.

Toolpath Guidelines

Before creating a Blend toolpath, consider the following:

  • Blend toolpaths are finishing toolpaths. In most cases, it is best practice to use them after roughing operations are complete.

  • 2D Blend Mill allows you to select machining geometry, but not avoidance geometry. Surface Finish Blend and 3D Blend allow you to select machining and avoidance geometry.

  • Blend operations require certain geometry selections for toolpath generation.

    • Blend Mill requires at least two selected Machining region chains.

    • Surface Finish Blend requires at least two selected Blend chains.

    • 3D Blend requires at least two selected Curves.

Types of Blend operations

Mastercam offers three types of Blend toolpaths: 3D HST Blend, Surface Finish Blend, and 2D Blend Mill.

Blend is a 3D high speed toolpath. This type of toolpath has machining strategies specially designed to produce the most efficient tool motion when machining surface models or solid faces. Surface Finish Blend is a surface finish/rough toolpath, typically designed to finish a part down to the drive geometry or the specified stock amount. The outcome of both toolpaths is typically similar, as shown below.

Blend Mill is a 2D high speed toolpath. This type of toolpath is a simple, but powerful and effective toolpath for specialized machining, such as machining pockets, faces or chamfers.

What are the differences between the toolpaths?

Group

Blend Mill belongs to the 2D high speed toolpaths, Surface Finish Blend belongs to the surface finish/rough toolpaths, and 3D Blend belongs to the 3D high speed toolpaths. The toolpath group affects the parameters, geometry selection, and cutting speed. Learn more about these toolpath types in 3D Mill and Router Toolpaths and 2D Mill and Router Toolpaths toolpaths.

Location

Access the 3D Blend and Blend Mill by clicking the Milling tab and selection the operation from either the 3D or 2D gallery. To access Surface Finish Blend, right-click in the Toolpaths Manager and select Mill toolpaths, Surface Finish, Blend.

Geometry Selection

Geometry selection for 3D HST Blend is similar to the other 3D HST operations. After selecting the toolpath from the ribbon, the toolpath parameters dialog box opens. Use the Model Geometry and Toolpath Control pages in the toolpath dialog box to set machining regions, containment boundaries, and avoidance regions.

For Surface Finish Blend, Mastercam prompts you to select drive geometry, check geometry, blend geometry, and a tool containment boundary. You can also select an approximate starting point. This process is similar to the geometry selection process for all surface finish/rough operations.

For Blend Mill, Mastercam prompts you to select machining geometry. You cannot select any other type of chain geometry. You can also use Automatic regions to have Mastercam automatically create machining regions for you based on the selected geometry.

Parameters

The following parameters are unique to each type of Blend operation:

  • The Flatten chains option for 3D HST Blend converts the selected Machining geometry into 2D/planar curves before the toolpath has been generated. Flattening the chains may shorten their length.

  • 3D HST Blend includes Project options for your selected Curves. This option works with the Contain option to further refine your toolpath. You can learn more about these options in the Toolpath Control page: Blend Help topic.

  • 3D HST Blend includes the Flip stepover option, which reverses the cutting direction of the toolpath. This option is found on the Cut Parameters page.

  • Surface Finish Blend includes the Blend Along Cut Settings dialog box, which allows you to change the resolution of the tool stepover when using the Along option.

  • Blend Mill includes an Extend Entry/Exit option, allowing you to adjust the initial and final tool engagement with the material. You can learn more about these options in the Cut Parameters page: Blend Mill Help topic.

Similar to how Blend operations share the geometry selection process of their toolpath group, Blend operations also share parameters with other toolpaths in their group.

  • 3D HST Blend, Surface Finish Blend, and 2D Blend Mill include different cut styles in the Cutting methods drop-down, based on their toolpath group.

  • 3D HST Blend operations have advanced linking parameter options, to give you more control over leads, retracts, and transitions. You can access these options on the Linking Parameters page.

  • Surface Finish Blend operations have Gap Settings that allow you to control what happens when a break in tool motion occurs in a Surface toolpath.

  • Blend Mill operations includes break through that allows you to cut completely through the material by a specified amount. Break through causes the tool to cut into the spoil board to ensure a clean cut through the material.

  • Blend Mill operations include a Finish Passes page that allows you to add high speed finishing passes along the selected boundaries of the toolpath.

  • To control the minimum and maximum machining depth for the toolpath, most Surface Finish operations use Depth Limits. Similarly, most 3D HST toolpaths allow you to enter a maximum and minimum Z depth value on the Steep/Shallow page to establish the heights of the highest and lowest cutting passes. Learn more in Using Z Limits to Create a Machining Zone. 2D HST toolpaths include the Depth Cuts page which is used to divide the total depth of a toolpath into smaller Z-axis cuts to reduce tool wear.

What are the similarities between the toolpaths?

The three blend toolpaths share the following parameters:

2D/3D

All Blend toolpaths include a 2D and 3D option (for 3D HST Blend, this option is the Project drop-down). This option affects how the created passes lie on the part. Selecting 2D keeps the passes equidistant in the Cplane. Selecting 3D keeps the passes equidistant in 3D, adding cuts in steep areas. The two examples below show 3D and 2D for a 3D high speed Blend toolpath.

Additionally, you can also choose between Across and Along cutting directions. Across creates cuts from one chain to the other, beginning at the start point of the first selected chain. Along creates cuts on the selected machining/drive geometry that follows along the chain direction, beginning at the start point of the first selected chain. The two examples below show Across and Along for a Blend Mill toolpath.

You can find these options in the Cut Parameters page or Finish blend parameters tab.

NOTE

Certain combinations of these options are not possible depending on your Blend toolpath. For example, Across is only available if you select 2D for Surface Finish Blend.

NOTE

3D HST Blend includes additional 2D and 3D controls for your selected Curves. You can learn more about these options on the Toolpath Control page: Blend Help topic.

How do I decide between using a 3D high speed toolpath or a Surface toolpath?

When deciding between using a high speed toolpath or a Surface Rough or Finish toolpath, consider the following:

  • Toolpaths may have options and parameters that are unique to their group. For example, Surface toolpaths have more options for entering/exiting the part, and 3D high speed toolpaths have more options for linking parameters.

  • 3D HST toolpaths support holder collision checking when needed.

  • 3D HST toolpaths better support the use of stock models when needed.

How do I decide between using a 2D toolpath or a 3D toolpath?

When deciding between using a 2D toolpath or a 3D toolpath, consider the following:

  • 2D toolpaths are best used for prismatic parts, meaning the walls of the part are generally vertical.

  • 3D toolpaths are best used for organic or mold/die parts.

  • 3D HST toolpaths support holder collision checking when needed.

What causes the Reporting computation error to display for 3D high speed Blend?

If you receive a computer error when generating a 3D high speed Blend toolpath, you may not have selected curves. The Blend toolpath requires at least two selected curves; the Curves parameter is located on the Toolpath Control page.

If you do have Curves selected and are receiving this error, other parameters might be incorrect.
