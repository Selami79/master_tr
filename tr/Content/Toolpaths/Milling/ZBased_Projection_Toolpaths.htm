Z-Based Projection Toolpaths

The Z-Based Projection toolpaths project a pattern lying in the XY plane of the tool plane down the Z axis onto the model. These toolpaths typically perform well on shallow areas, as the distance between pattern passes can vary when projected onto steep features. However, some toolpaths contain advanced options to improve the results on steep areas.

Z-Based Projection toolpaths consist of the following toolpaths:

  • Surface Finish and Surface Rough Parallel
  • Surface Finish, Surface Rough, and 3D High Speed Radial
  • Surface Finish, Surface Rough, and 3D High Speed Project
  • 3D High Speed Raster
  • 3D High Speed Spiral
Toolpath guidelines

The Z-Based Projection toolpaths contain a mix of roughing and finishing toolpaths. In most cases, it is best practice to create a finishing operation after a roughing operation. Additionally, keep the following in mind when creating a Radial or Spiral toolpath.

  • Radial toolpaths can have problems with overmachining in the area where the cutting passes converge. In these cases, you can restrict the tool motion by specifying an inner radius. Mastercam will not calculate cutting passes for the area of the circle inside this radius. These parameters are found on the Cut Parameters page.

  • When creating a Spiral toolpath, you must enter the outer radius of the spiral and the coordinates of its center point. This is different from a Waterline toolpath, where each cutting pass represents the actual profile of the drive surface at a particular Z depth.

Types of Z-Based Projection operations

There are ten Z-Based Projection toolpaths: three finishing toolpaths and seven roughing toolpaths. All toolpaths project a pattern down the Z axis, but the type of pattern depends on your chosen toolpath.

Project

The Project toolpaths project curves, points, or another toolpath (NCI) file onto surfaces or solids. Projecting points onto surfaces can create plunging motion to clear small areas in detailed parts. Surface Rough Project can clean out cavities using a pocketing motion generated by a standard pocket projected onto the surfaces that make up the cavity. Point projection can create plunge-like motion with precise location of the points. Rough plunge, by contrast, creates the plunge points in a grid or along the path of a previously created operation.

Radial

The Radial toolpath machine from a center point outward, creating cuts like the spokes of a wheel. These toolpaths are best suited for rough parts. For 3D high speed Radial toolpaths, this machining strategy is most effective on areas with shallow curved surfaces and circular areas. Since the stepover between each pass is a 2D value calculated in the XY plane only, the cuts might not be appropriate for steep areas. This is especially true when the steep contour is perpendicular to the cutting direction. In such areas a Waterline, Spiral, or Hybrid toolpath might produce better results.

 

Parallel

The Parallel toolpath quickly remove stock using multiple, constant Z depth moves. Finish Parallel makes cuts directly on the selected machining surface.

Raster

The Raster toolpath creates a set of parallel passes with a stepover along a line at a set angle. This toolpath is most effective on shallow, nearly horizontal surfaces, or steeper surfaces that are perpendicular to the angle of the passes.

Spiral

The Spiral toolpath creates cutting passes where the tool feeds into the part in a continuous spiral instead of several discrete passes at a constant Z height. The spacing between each pass is a 2D distance measured in the XY plane, so this toolpath type works best on shallow parts whose features can be effectively machined with a circular motion. What are the differences between the toolpaths?

Group

3D high speed Raster, Radial, Project, and Spiral belong to the 3D high speed toolpaths and Surface Finish/Rough Parallel, Radial, and Project belong to the surface finish/rough toolpaths. The toolpath group affects the parameters, geometry selection, and cutting speed.

Location

Access 3D HST Radial, Raster, Spiral, and Project by clicking the Milling tab and selecting the operation from the 3D gallery.

To access Surface Finish/Rough Radial, Project, and Parallel, right-click in the Toolpaths Manager and select the toolpath from the Mill toolpaths, Surface Finish or Surface Rough menu.

Geometry Selection

Geometry selection for 3D high speed Raster, Radial, Spiral, and Project is similar to the other 3D HST operations. After selecting the toolpath from the ribbon, the toolpath parameters dialog box opens. Use the Model Geometry and Toolpath Control pages in the toolpath dialog box to set machining regions and containment boundaries.

For the surface finish/rough operations, Mastercam prompts you to select drive geometry, check geometry, and a tool containment boundary. You can also select an approximate starting point. This process is similar to the selection process for all surface finish/rough operations.

Parameters

  • Rough Project, Finish Project, and 3D high speed Project include options on the Cut parameters page or Project parameters tab to set how you project curves, points, or another toolpath (NCI file) onto surfaces or solids.

  • 3D high speed Project includes the Order by depth option, which controls the depth cut order. Deselect to order by input entry.

  • Spiral includes a Spiral clockwise option on the Cut Parameters page, which sets the direction of the spiral. This does not change the starting point of the spiral, only its direction.

  • You can limit the machining zone by specifying a starting and ending angle for 3D high speed Radial. The start and end angle are positive values measured from the X-positive axis.

  • Raster toolpaths include the Machining angle and Perpendicular fill options on the Cut parameters page. Machining angle orients the cutting passes. Perpendicular fill, in conjunction with Custom, limits passes relative to 1.4 times the Stepover.

  • If you have selected an Approximate start point for 3D high speed Raster or Radial, once an area has been machined the tool moves to the start point of the closest unmachined area.

  • 3D high speed Raster includes a Cut order option on the Cut parameters page. This allows you to order the cuts by clusters or lanes.

  • 3D high speed Radial and Spiral include the Center point, Inner radius, and Outer radius parameters on the Cut parameters page. Center point sets the XY coordinates of the center point of machining. Inner radius and Outer radius are used to create the cutting passes.

Similar to how Z-Based Projection operations share the geometry selection process of their toolpath group, these operations also share parameters with other toolpaths in their group.

  • The 3D high speed toolpaths have more options for Cutting method than the Surface Finish and Rough operations. Other 3D high speed toolpaths share these advanced options. However, 3D high speed Project does not have a Cutting method option.

  • 3D high speed toolpaths have advanced linking parameter options to give you more control over leads, retracts, and transitions. You can access these options on the Linking Parameters page.

  • The surface finish and rough operations include a Gap Settings dialog box that allows you to control what happens when a break in tool motion occurs.

  • To control the minimum and maximum machining depth for the toolpath, most Surface Finish operations use the Depth Limits dialog box. Similarly, most 3D HST toolpaths allow you to enter a maximum and minimum Z depth value on the Steep/Shallow page to establish the heights of the highest and lowest cutting passes. Learn more in Using Z Limits to Create a Machining Zone.

What are the similarities between the toolpaths?

In addition to all being pattern-projection toolpaths, the following parameters are shared between multiple toolpath types:

  • Surface Rough Project, Radial, and Pencil include the Allow negative/positive Z motion along surface option on the toolpath parameters tab. These options let the tool cut along the surface while plunging.

  • 3D HST Spiral, Radial, and Raster include Down up/mill options, Overlap and Shallow angle. Overlap ensures that the toolpath does not leave unwanted scallops or cusps in the transition areas between passes of different directions. Shallow angle creates down or up milling passes in areas where the Machining geometry is almost flat. These options are on the Cut Parameters page.

  • Surface Rough Parallel, Radial, and Project include options for plunge control. Plunge control determines the type of Z-axis movements for surface rough toolpaths. Learn more in Plunge Control: Surface Rough Toolpaths. This option is also shared with Surface Rough Flowline.

How do I decide between using a 3D high speed toolpath or a Surface toolpath?

When deciding between using a high speed toolpath or a Surface Rough or Finish toolpath, consider the following:

  • Toolpaths may have options and parameters that are unique to their group. For example, Surface toolpaths have more options for entering/exiting the part, and 3D high speed toolpaths have more options for linking parameters.

  • 3D HST toolpaths support holder collision checking when needed.

  • 3D HST toolpaths better support the use of stock models when needed.
