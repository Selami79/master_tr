Surface Reflection and Materials Mapping

Use surface reflection and materials mapping to create striking proofs that will help your customer visualize the finished part.

  • Material bitmaps are bitmapped images that are stored locally in your Mastercam installation folder. When you apply a material bitmap to your Art base surface (ABS), Art projects the bitmap onto the ABS which gives your ABS the visual qualities of that material. By setting the parameters in the dialog box, you can precisely control the size, rotation, placement, and opacity (power) of the bitmap as you map it to your surface.
  • Reflection bitmaps are also bitmapped images that are stored locally in your Mastercam installation folder. However, in contrast to material bitmaps, they are not projected on to the Art surface. Instead, the reflection image surrounds the ABS, and each facet of the ABS independently reflects the image back. The reflection image may even bounce from one plane of the ABS to another, creating a more realistic result.
Material bitmaps vs. Reflection bitmaps

We can see the difference between these two settings by applying them separately to an ABS.

For this example we will use the sample part, SHIELD-ART_M.mcam and the bitmap, Metal brushed_256.png (stored locally in both the reflection bitmaps and the material bitmaps folder).

In the illustration below, the bitmap is applied to the part as a material. Scroll to the second image to view the part with the bitmap re-applied as a reflection.

Notice how much more distinct the brush marks are on the first example. Although setting the reflection power to 100% gives you the sharpest possible reflection, the bitmap in the second example is reflected off each plane of the curved and complex surface, changing and distorting its appearance.

The material bitmap is actually mapped on to the Art surface, preserving the image's detail. We can further refine our settings and precisely place the bitmap on the ABS using tiling, offset, and rotation. Opacity

Combining surface reflection and materials mapping with opacity can produce the effect of glass or precious stones. In this example, a chrome ring is transformed into glass simply by reducing the surface opacity.
